# From Barracks to Box Tops: The 77-Year Marine Corps Tradition Behind Toys for Tots

**[SUBHEAD]**
*How a Marine's wife and a handmade doll started America's largest toy collection program*

---

**[LEAD]**
In 1947, Diane Hendricks couldn't find an organization to accept the handmade doll she'd made for a child in need. So she asked her husband, Marine Corps Reserve Major Bill Hendricks, for help.

His solution? Put a collection box at the local movie theater in Los Angeles. That single box collected 5,000 toys that first year.

Seventy-seven years later, Toys for Tots has distributed over 600 million toys to more than 281 million children - and it remains an official activity of the United States Marine Corps Reserve.

---

**[SECTION 1: THE FOUNDING STORY]**

### A Doll That Started a Movement

The story begins with Diane Hendricks' craftwork. She'd made a Raggedy Ann doll to donate to a needy child, but couldn't find an organization to accept it. When she mentioned this to her husband, Bill, he saw an opportunity.

Major Hendricks coordinated with other Marine Reservists to place collection boxes at movie theaters, fire stations, and businesses across Los Angeles. The response was overwhelming.

That first campaign's success caught the attention of Marine Corps leadership. In 1948, the Commandant directed all Marine Reserve units to conduct Toys for Tots campaigns. A national program was born.

The connection to the Marine Corps wasn't accidental. The values aligned: commitment to community, service beyond self, and taking care of those who need it most. If Marines were willing to defend the nation, surely they could help ensure every child had a Christmas gift.

---

**[SECTION 2: GROWING INTO A NATIONAL FORCE]**

### From Local Campaign to National Institution

What started with one collection box grew exponentially:

**The 1950s:** Expansion across California, then nationwide as Marine Reserve units adopted the program.

**The 1960s:** Celebrity involvement brought national attention. John Wayne, Bob Hope, and other stars championed the cause.

**The 1970s-80s:** Corporate partnerships developed, providing toy donations and funding at scale.

**The 1990s:** The Marine Toys for Tots Foundation was established to raise funds and support local campaigns year-round.

**2000s-Present:** Digital transformation brought online donations, social media engagement, and expanded reach into underserved communities.

Today, Toys for Tots operates 800+ local campaigns in all 50 states, the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands. Each campaign is coordinated by local Marines - active and veteran - who volunteer their time.

---

**[SECTION 3: WHY THE MARINE CORPS]**

### The Connection That Makes It Work

Why does a toy donation program belong with the Marine Corps? The relationship goes deeper than history:

**Built-in infrastructure:**
Marine Reserve units exist in communities across America. Each unit provides a ready-made volunteer base, leadership structure, and logistical capability.

**Trust and credibility:**
The Marine Corps is one of America's most trusted institutions. When people see "U.S. Marine Corps Reserve" associated with a charity, they donate with confidence.

**Leadership development:**
Coordinating a Toys for Tots campaign teaches Marines community engagement, logistics, and leadership skills that translate to military and civilian careers.

**Continuing service:**
For Marine veterans, Toys for Tots offers a way to continue serving after active duty ends. Many campaign coordinators are retired Marines.

**Mission alignment:**
Marines protect the nation's future. Children are the nation's future. The connection is natural.

---

**[SECTION 4: BY THE NUMBERS]**

### 77 Years of Impact

The scale of Toys for Tots' achievement is remarkable:

- **600+ million toys** distributed since 1947
- **281+ million children** served
- **800+ local campaigns** operating annually
- **7+ million children** reached each holiday season
- **11,000+ Marines** volunteering annually
- **All 50 states** covered

These aren't just statistics. Each number represents a child who woke up Christmas morning with something to unwrap, a family that experienced holiday normalcy despite financial hardship, and a community that came together to take care of its own.

---

**[SECTION 5: THE MODERN PROGRAM]**

### Toys for Tots Today

While the mission remains unchanged, the program has evolved:

**Digital giving:**
Online donations, virtual toy drives, and social media campaigns complement traditional collection boxes.

**Native American outreach:**
Specific programs reach children on remote reservations where standard delivery doesn't work.

**Military family support:**
The program serves not just civilian families in need, but also military families facing deployment-related hardships.

While peak activity is October-December, the foundation works year-round on fundraising, logistics, and community relationships.

Bill Hendricks passed away in 2002, but the movement he and Diane started continues to grow. What began with a handmade doll and a question - "How can I help?" - has become America's largest toy collection program.

Every toy dropped in a Toys for Tots box carries forward a 77-year tradition of Marines serving communities, neighbors helping neighbors, and Americans ensuring every child experiences holiday joy.

**Volunteer opportunities:**
Organize team outings to sort or distribute toys. These experiences create stronger engagement than just dropping off donations.

**Progress visibility:**
Post real-time progress toward goals. Seeing momentum builds more momentum.

**Recognition:**
Feature top donors in company communications. Public recognition drives behavior.

---

**[SECTION 5: AVOIDING COMMON MISTAKES]**

### What to Watch Out For

**Mistake: Starting too late**
Drives announced in December often fizzle. Start planning in September, launch by early November.

**Mistake: Poor location**
Collection boxes in a basement closet won't get donations. Place them where employees naturally pass - lobbies, break rooms, elevators.

**Mistake: One-and-done communication**
Single email announcements get lost. Plan a communication cadence with multiple touchpoints.

**Mistake: No goal or tracking**
"Please donate toys" is less motivating than "Help us reach 500 toys - we're at 423!" Share progress frequently.

**Mistake: Excluding remote workers**
Remote employees can participate through online donations, virtual toy drives, or matching gifts. Don't leave them out.

**Mistake: Forgetting the why**
Connect the drive to real impact. Share stories about families served. Make it tangible.

### How to Know If Your Drive Worked

**Quantitative metrics:**
- Total toys collected
- Dollar value of donations
- Participation rate (% of employees)
- Comparison to previous years

**Qualitative indicators:**
- Employee feedback and sentiment
- Social media engagement
- Volunteer event attendance
- Interest in future programs

**External recognition:**
- Local media coverage
- Charity partner feedback
- Community reputation impact

Document these metrics to build the case for continued or expanded programs.
