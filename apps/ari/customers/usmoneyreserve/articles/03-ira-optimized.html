A complete guide to adding physical gold and silver to your retirement portfolio through a self-directed IRA

Gold prices remain near historic highs amid ongoing inflation concerns, prompting many Americans to consider precious metals for retirement portfolios. A Gold IRA is a self-directed Individual Retirement Account that holds physical gold, silver, platinum, or palladium instead of traditional stocks and bonds.[2][6]

Gold IRAs follow IRS rules, costs, and considerations that differ from standard retirement accounts. This guide covers key details before adding precious metals to a retirement strategy.

What Is a Gold IRA?

A Gold IRA is a self-directed IRA that holds physical precious metals rather than paper assets. Unlike conventional IRAs limited to stocks, bonds, and mutual funds, self-directed IRAs permit IRS-approved gold, silver coins, and bars.[2][4]

The key feature is ownership of actual metal, stored in an approved depository. This differs from gold ETFs or mining stocks, as it involves physical bullion held in the account owner's name.[1]

Types of Gold IRAs

Gold IRAs come in two primary types:

  • Traditional Gold IRA: Contributions may be tax-deductible. Taxes apply on withdrawals in retirement.[2]
  • Roth Gold IRA: Contributions use after-tax dollars. Qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.[1][2]

The choice depends on current versus expected retirement tax brackets, similar to standard IRA decisions.[2]

IRS Rules for Gold IRAs

The IRS imposes strict requirements on Gold IRAs. Violations can lead to taxes, penalties, or account disqualification.[3][4]

Approved Metals

Only metals meeting minimum purity qualify: - Gold: 99.5% pure (except American Gold Eagles at 91.67%, which are exempted).[3] - Silver: 99.9% pure.[3] - Platinum and palladium: 99.95% pure.[3][4]

Common eligible products include American Gold Eagles, American Silver Eagles, Canadian Maple Leafs, Australian Kangaroos, and bars from approved refiners.[3]

Storage and Custody Requirements

  • Custodian required: An IRS-approved custodian, such as a bank or trust company, must manage the metals. Personal holding is not allowed.[2][4]
  • Depository storage: Metals must remain in an IRS-approved depository. Home storage disqualifies the account.[4]

Contribution Limits and RMDs

  • Contributions follow IRA limits: $7,500 for 2026 ($8,600 if age 50 or older).[5][6]
  • Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs): Traditional Gold IRAs require distributions starting at age 73. Owners typically sell metals for cash or take in-kind distributions.[2]

How to Set Up a Gold IRA

Setting up a Gold IRA requires coordination among the account owner, a dealer, custodian, and depository. The process typically takes 2-3 weeks.[2]

Step-by-Step Process

  • Step 1: Select a dealer. The dealer assists with product selection and setup. Companies like U.S. Money Reserve provide guidance through their Gold Standard IRA program.[original]
  • Step 2: Choose a custodian. The custodian ensures IRS compliance and holds the account.[2]
  • Step 3: Open the IRA. Submit paperwork including identification and beneficiary details.[2]
  • Step 4: Fund the account.
    • Rollover from 401(k) or similar (direct preferred to avoid taxes).[2][7]
    • Transfer from another IRA.
    • New contribution within limits.[6]
  • Step 5: Select metals. Choose IRS-approved products, such as a mix of gold and silver coins or bars.[3]
  • Step 6: Arrange storage. Opt for segregated or commingled storage in an approved depository.[2]

Gold IRA Fees

Gold IRAs incur costs beyond standard IRAs:

  • Setup fees: $50-$150 one-time.[original]
  • Annual custodian fees: $75-$300.[original]
  • Storage fees: 0.5%-1% of value or $100-$300 flat annually.[original]
  • Dealer premiums: Markup over spot price, varying by product.[original]
  • Transaction fees: Per purchase or sale, if applicable.[original]

Total annual costs often range from $200-$500 for moderate accounts. Request a full fee schedule in writing.[original]

Choosing a Gold IRA Dealer

Dealer selection affects the process and costs. Consider these factors:

  • Educational resources explaining Gold IRA rules and options.
  • Transparent pricing on fees and premiums.
  • Range of IRS-approved products from recognized mints.
  • Buyback programs for liquidation.
  • Dedicated customer service representatives.

Red Flags

  • Pressure tactics or "limited availability" claims.
  • Advice on home storage (prohibited).[4]
  • Unusually low fees suggesting hidden costs.
  • Lack of cost transparency.[3]

U.S. Money Reserve, for example, assigns dedicated account executives and offers the Gold Standard IRA program with step-by-step guidance.[original]

Is a Gold IRA Right for Retirement?

Gold IRAs provide diversification but suit specific needs.

When to Consider

  • Seeking alternatives to stocks and bonds.
  • Hedging inflation risks.
  • Long-term holding (decades).[1]
  • Preference for tangible assets.
  • Tolerance for higher fees.[3]

When to Avoid

  • Need for high liquidity.
  • Short time to withdrawals.
  • Priority on lowest costs.
  • Hands-off management preference.[3]

Advisors often recommend 5-15% allocation to precious metals for diversification without overexposure. Gold IRAs complement, not replace, broader portfolios.[original]
